# Casting and character design

Use this reference to turn a locked dramatic role into an original, producible character identity and a controlled set of appearance, performance, and voice references.

## Role boundary

The **casting director** owns the casting brief, ensemble contrast, performance requirements, and reference-rights check. The **character-design and wardrobe director** owns the original visual identity, silhouette, grooming, wardrobe system, and stage-specific variants. The **performance director** owns playable behavior. The **continuity supervisor** owns version records. An `authorized_approver` is the only party who may lock a candidate.

Do not use casting or appearance design to rewrite a locked character arc. Return story contradictions to the screenwriter or showrunner.

## Required inputs

Read the current project bible and obtain:

- role function, objective, opposition, arc, relationships, and hidden information;
- age range or age presentation, period, location, occupation, class conditions, and lived experience;
- required physical actions, emotional range, dialogue or voice demands, and special states;
- audience profile, tone, visual medium, production constraints, and downstream tools if known;
- existing locked characters, scene palette, and rights or consent records.

If a missing choice would materially change identity, present bounded options and wait for the authorized decision. Do not silently invent a public-figure reference.

## Casting workflow

### 1. Write the dramatic casting brief

Describe observable requirements rather than celebrity shorthand:

- **story function**: what the audience must understand on first appearance;
- **life imprint**: age presentation, occupation, environment, habits, fatigue, privilege, hardship, training, or injury that should be visible;
- **performance range**: restraint, volatility, comedy, authority, vulnerability, action, intimacy, dialect, or specialist behavior;
- **contradiction**: the useful gap between surface impression and inner drive;
- **relationship energy**: how the character should contrast with partners, rivals, family, and groups;
- **production demands**: action, prosthetics, age states, wardrobe changes, voice continuity, or repeated close-ups;
- **exclusions**: stereotypes, confusing similarity to another cast member, and any unlicensed likeness or voice direction.

Do not infer morality, intelligence, class, or personality from facial structure, skin tone, disability, ethnicity, or body type. Connect design choices to story history and observable behavior, not physiognomy.

### 2. Check ensemble readability

Build a small comparison matrix before generating individual candidates:

| Character | First-read function | Silhouette | Face / hair contrast | Movement rhythm | Voice contrast | Relationship contrast |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| [ID] | [fill] | [fill] | [fill] | [fill] | [fill] | [fill] |

Revise any pair that could be confused in the intended framing, lighting, wardrobe, or audio conditions. Contrast may come from posture, timing, styling, behavior, or voice; it does not require exaggerated anatomy.

### 3. Design an original identity

Use neutral, non-ranking vocabulary:

- age presentation and cultural or regional context when relevant to the story;
- face shape and relative feature geometry;
- hair shape, texture, color, and maintenance level;
- complexion and skin texture without treating one tone as more refined or desirable;
- body proportions, posture, gait, gesture scale, and resting tension;
- one or two stable, production-feasible identifiers;
- default grooming and wardrobe logic derived from means, work, climate, period, and self-image.

Avoid “perfect,” “superior,” “exotic,” “high-class face,” or other value labels presented as anatomy. Avoid designing every sympathetic character as conventionally beautiful or every antagonist as visually harsh. Counter-casting is valid when it strengthens the story.

### 4. Define the reference set

Request only the views needed by the production. A useful identity set may include:

1. neutral-light face close-up;
2. front, three-quarter, and profile views;
3. full-body silhouette and neutral posture;
4. one expression range sheet;
5. one approved baseline wardrobe state;
6. only the stage, injury, age, disguise, or ceremonial variants scheduled for production.

Keep lighting, lens impression, grooming, and identity consistent across views. Record which image controls face identity, body and posture, wardrobe, color, or expression. A full-body image alone is not a sufficient face reference.

### 5. Generate bounded candidates

Generate a reviewable set rather than an unlimited search. Keep each candidate in `proposal` status and assign a stable candidate ID. Never place a candidate into downstream scene, storyboard, or video references before approval unless the authorized approver has explicitly allowed a provisional test.

### 6. Self-review before human approval

Reject or revise a candidate that fails any of these checks:

- role function and lived experience are not legible;
- identity drifts across views or resembles another locked character;
- age presentation, anatomy, hands, posture, wardrobe, or cultural details are implausible;
- the design depends on small accessories that are unlikely to remain continuous;
- the candidate creates an unintended real-person resemblance;
- reference source, likeness consent, voice consent, or license scope is missing;
- the image contains unrequested text, logo, watermark, private data, or protected design;
- the candidate is aesthetically polished but does not support the required performance.

Self-review may remove an invalid candidate from consideration. It may not lock a candidate.

### 7. Obtain authorized approval and lock

Present the surviving candidates with a short, comparative rationale. The authorized approver may `approve`, `revise`, `reject`, or authorize a named provisional test.

On approval:

1. copy or promote the approved asset without overwriting the candidate;
2. assign a stable character and look-state ID;
3. record the approved version, approver, date, source, consent or license scope, and intended use;
4. update the character card and project bible;
5. mark alternative candidates `rejected` or `superseded` according to the retention policy;
6. allow downstream roles to reference only the locked version.

Generation success is not creative approval. Agent preference is not authorized approval.

## Likeness, voice, and reference rights

- Create original fictional identities by default.
- Do not request, construct, or preserve a recognizable imitation of a public figure, celebrity, politician, performer, creator, or other known person.
- Do not evade this rule by omitting a name, mixing signature features, stylizing a photograph, converting it to illustration, or using indirect descriptors.
- Use a private person's face, body, performance, or voice only when the project records clear authorization or consent for the intended scope. Confirm medium, purpose, territory, duration, alteration rights, and whether synthetic derivatives or voice cloning are permitted.
- A reference found online is not automatically licensed. Record the source and applicable license or permission.
- Do not clone, imitate, or synthesize a recognizable voice without authorization. A text description of a generic vocal quality is safer than an identity reference.
- Do not expose private source material, embedded metadata, access tokens, contact information, or consent documents in prompts or public deliverables.
- If authorization is absent or ambiguous, replace the reference with an original direction. Do not guess that use is fair, harmless, or allowed.

These records support production governance; they are not a legal ruling or a substitute for professional advice in the relevant jurisdiction.

## Wardrobe and state design

Lock a small wardrobe system, not every outfit:

- baseline daily state;
- work, duty, or public-facing state;
- story-driven status or identity transition;
- scheduled special event, disguise, injury, weather, or ceremonial state.

For each state, record narrative purpose, silhouette, layer order, material, palette, footwear, grooming, makeup, dirt or wetness, interactive props, and continuity risks. Prefer a few stable identifiers over many tiny accessories. When a small object is story-critical, register it as a prop with its own continuity state.

## Voice and performance anchors

Describe behavior that can be performed and observed:

- resting posture, gesture scale, movement tempo, and use of personal space;
- eye-contact pattern and first response under pressure;
- what changes when the character lies, loses control, feels safe, or gains authority;
- speech pace, resonance, articulation, pauses, and relationship-specific exceptions;
- pronunciation or dialect requirements that need qualified review.

Do not reduce a character to repeated tics. One motivated exception is often more revealing than constant mannerisms.

## Handoff to other departments

Deliver:

- locked character card and stable asset IDs;
- approved identity, body, wardrobe, expression, and voice references with explicit responsibilities;
- authorization or consent status and usage limits;
- stage-state table and prohibited drift list;
- performance anchors and any specialist verification needs;
- rejected or provisional candidates clearly separated from canon.

The art director receives palette and material implications. The performance director receives playable behavior. The cinematography director receives only approved identity and state references. The continuity supervisor records all version transitions.

## Acceptance gate

Do not mark the character package `locked` until:

- the role is distinct within the ensemble;
- identity and stage variants are internally continuous;
- appearance choices serve story and performance rather than stereotypes;
- reference responsibilities are explicit;
- likeness, voice, and source authorization are recorded;
- the authorized approver has confirmed the exact version.
